China Apparel & Soft Goods Sourcing Guide: From Sample to Consistent Bulk

A practical reference for sourcing clothing, childrenswear, and soft goods from China: supplier types, fabric and trim control, sample-to-bulk consistency, and the checks that matter before payment.

Apparel is the category where a great sample and a bad shipment look identical in photos. The fabric swaps, the stitching loosens, the sizing drifts, the trims get cheaper — and none of it shows until the goods land. This guide is a reference for planning apparel and soft-goods sourcing so the bulk order matches the sample you approved.

Why apparel risk lives between sample and bulk

For most products, the risk is in choosing the right supplier. In apparel, that is only half of it. The other half is controlling consistency across a production run that may involve different fabric lots, multiple sewing lines, and components the factory buys from outside. A supplier can make a beautiful sample by hand and still deliver an inconsistent bulk order — not always from dishonesty, but from loose specs and no checkpoints.

  • Fabric substitution. The same "cotton" can vary in weight (GSM), blend, and finish. A lighter fabric cuts cost and changes how the garment looks and lasts.
  • Trim and accessory swaps. Zippers, buttons, drawcords, labels, and packaging are easy places to quietly downgrade between sample and bulk.
  • Grading and fit drift. A correct sample size does not guarantee the full size run is graded correctly. This is where return rates are made.

The tech pack is your real contract

In apparel, the document that controls your outcome is the tech pack, not the price quote. The more precise it is, the less room there is for the bulk order to drift. At minimum, pin down in writing:

  • Fabric: composition, weight (GSM), finish, and an approved physical swatch.
  • Measurements: a full size chart with tolerances, not just one sample size.
  • Trims and labels: exact components, brands or specs, and care-label requirements for your market.
  • Construction: stitch type, seam finish, and any reinforcement points.

The practical rule: approve a pre-production sample (PPS) made from the actual bulk fabric and trims — not the early showroom sample. The PPS is what you check the shipment against. Skipping it is the most common reason "the sample was great but the order was wrong."

Childrenswear and soft goods: extra compliance

If you sell childrenswear in the US or EU, safety compliance is not optional and is category-specific: drawcord and small-parts rules, flammability standards, and chemical testing (e.g. for restricted substances). Confirm which standards apply to your market and get the test reports tied to your actual fabric and trims before committing to bulk. This is cheaper to handle before production than after a shipment is held or recalled.

How to position your apparel sourcing

The strongest position in apparel is not "I found a cheaper factory." It is "I locked the fabric, trims, grading, and a bulk-fabric pre-production sample before the run started, and I check the shipment against that." In a category where the sample and the shipment can look the same in a photo, that control is the difference between a repeatable product line and a pile of returns.
