China Machinery & Industrial Equipment Sourcing Guide: Verifying Real Capability

A practical reference for sourcing machinery and industrial equipment from China: supplier capability, documentation, payment staging, and the verification that matters before a large commitment.

Machinery and industrial equipment is the highest-stakes sourcing category covered here. The orders are large, the lead times are long, the products are often semi-custom, and a mistake is not a cheap return — it is a wrong machine sitting in a container. This guide is a reference for sourcing equipment from China where the cost of getting it wrong is high enough that verification is not optional.

Why machinery sourcing is a different game

Consumer-goods logic does not transfer here. With equipment, you are buying capability and documentation as much as a finished product, and several factors raise the stakes:

  • Large, staged payments. Deposits and progress payments mean real money goes in before you ever see a finished machine. How payment is staged is part of the risk.
  • Semi-custom builds. Voltage, dimensions, capacity, and interface specs are often built to your requirement — which means a spec misunderstanding becomes an expensive rebuild.
  • A broker layer. Many "manufacturers" are trading companies or integrators assembling parts. For equipment, knowing who actually builds and stands behind the machine matters for warranty and support.
  • After-sales reality. Spare parts, technical support, and warranty mean little if you cannot reach the actual maker after shipment.

Capability and documentation: what to verify

AreaWhat to Confirm
Real manufacturing Live video of the workshop and the relevant production line; evidence they have built this machine type before, not just brochures
Technical spec Voltage and frequency for your market, dimensions, capacity, materials, and interface — agreed in a written technical agreement, not chat messages
Certification CE or other market-required marks with real test reports; safety and compliance for the destination country
Acceptance A factory acceptance test (FAT) defining how the machine is tested and signed off before shipment
After-sales Spare-parts availability, technical support channel, warranty terms, and installation or commissioning support

Payment staging is risk control, not paperwork

For equipment, how you stage payment is one of your main protections. A common structure ties payments to verifiable milestones rather than the calendar:

  • Deposit to start the build.
  • Progress payment against documented build milestones you can verify (photos, video, or an inspection).
  • Balance against a successful factory acceptance test, before shipment.

The practical rule: never let the final payment clear before a factory acceptance test confirms the machine meets the written technical agreement. The FAT and a milestone-based payment schedule are what stand between you and a wrong machine already on the water.

The broker question matters more here

In low-value categories, a trading layer is mostly a margin question. In equipment, it is a support question. If the company you paid did not build the machine and cannot service it, your warranty and technical support are only as good as a relationship you do not control. Confirming whether you are dealing with the actual manufacturer or a broker is one of the highest-value checks in this category.

How to position your machinery sourcing

The strongest position in equipment sourcing is not "I found a lower quote." It is "I verified the factory has built this machine before, agreed the technical spec in writing, staged payment against milestones, and required a factory acceptance test before the balance and shipment." When the downside is a wrong machine in a container, that process is the whole job.
